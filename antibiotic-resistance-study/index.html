History of Antibiotic Discovery HIGH YIELD

Before antibiotics, infectious diseases were the leading cause of death. Two key figures changed everything.

Paul Ehrlich (1854–1915) — The "Magic Bullet" Pioneer

  • Born in Poland, earned doctorate studying tissue staining with dyes (foundational for pathology)
  • Won Nobel Prize in 1908 for contributions to immunology (serum therapy for diphtheria)
  • Hypothesized he could stain bacteria → successfully stained Mycobacterium tuberculosis
  • His staining work led Hans Christian Gram to develop the Gram Stain technique
  • Theorized "magic bullets" — chemicals that would exclusively target the parasite within the organism
  • With Sahachiro Hata, screened compounds against syphilis (Treponema pallidum, discovered 1905)
  • Found Salvarsan and Neosalvarsan — could treat syphilis
  • Not considered a true antibiotic — he is considered one of the founders of chemotherapy

Alexander Fleming (1881–1955) — The Discoverer of Penicillin

  • Born in Scotland, served as captain in WWI Medical Corps — saw soldiers die from infected wounds
  • "Not a very careful scientist" — this actually led to TWO major discoveries:

Discovery #1: Lysozyme (1922)

  • Had a cold → plated some of his mucus → forgot about it for 2 weeks
  • Found bacteria everywhere on the plate except where his mucus was
  • Discovered this antimicrobial substance was also in saliva and tears
  • Named it lysozyme (1922)

Discovery #2: Penicillin (1928)

  • Was experimenting with Staphylococcus aureus → left an open plate by an open window → went on vacation
  • Came back and found the plate contaminated with a fungus
  • Noticed S. aureus colonies were dying near the edge of the fungus
  • Identified the fungus as Penicillium
  • Diluted the fungus in culture → inhibited gram-positive bacteria up to 800-fold dilution
  • Theorized it was some "juice" the mold produced → named it penicillin
  • Could kill agents of scarlet fever, pneumonia, gonorrhea, meningitis, diphtheria
  • Published results in 1929 — to little interest
  • Argued for penicillin from 1929 on but couldn't produce large enough quantities
  • Howard Florey and Ernst Chain helped him mass-produce penicillin
  • At onset of WWII → mass-produced in the US by Pfizer, Merck, Abbott, and others
  • Fleming, Florey, and Chain won the Nobel Prize in 1945
Context: Before penicillin, infectious disease was the #1 killer. After mass production, deaths from infection plummeted. This is visible in the leading causes of death charts from 1900s vs 2000s.

What Is an Antibiotic? HIGH YIELD

DEF An antibiotic is a medicine that inhibits the growth of or destroys microorganisms.

Antibiotics can clear a bacterial infection but may NOT actually kill the bacteria!

Two Types

TypeDefinitionHow It Works
BactericidalCan directly kill the bacteriaTargets essential structures/processes so cell dies
BacteriostaticCannot directly kill, but stops growthHalts bacterial replication; host immune system then clears the pathogen

What Makes a Good Antibiotic?

  • Clears bacterial infections
  • Broad spectrum = targets many species of bacteria (more useful)
  • Narrow spectrum = only targets a few species
  • Not toxic to humans — achieved by targeting prokaryotic-specific structures
  • Effective at a physiologically achievable dose
  • Can be easily delivered (oral preferred, also IV)

Targets of Antibiotics (5 General Mechanisms) HIGH YIELD

Most antibiotics target one of 5 prokaryotic-specific targets. This is how they avoid harming human cells.

#TargetWhy It Works
1Cell WallHuman cells don't have cell walls; bacteria need peptidoglycan to survive
2DNA/RNA SynthesisBacterial DNA replication and transcription machinery differs from eukaryotic
3Folate SynthesisBacteria must synthesize their own folate; humans get it from diet
4Prokaryotic Cell MembraneBacterial membranes have different composition than human membranes
5Protein SynthesisProkaryotic ribosomes (70S) are different from eukaryotic (80S); many ways to inhibit them
Key concept: Antibiotics can target the SAME pathway at DIFFERENT points. For example, there are many different antibiotics that inhibit protein synthesis, each with a unique mechanism of action.

Mechanism of Action = the biochemical process through which a drug produces its effect

  • Each antibiotic has its own specific mechanism of action
  • Multiple antibiotics can target the same general pathway but hit different steps
  • Protein synthesis is the most commonly targeted because the prokaryotic ribosome has many vulnerable points

Testing for Antibiotic Susceptibility HIGH YIELD

The goal of infectious disease care: identify which antibiotic works and at what dose.

The Process

1. Collect bacteria from the patient (culture) → grow on culture media 2. Expose bacteria to different antibiotics 3. Check for results: No growth = bacteria is susceptible (antibiotic works) Growth = bacteria is resistant (antibiotic doesn't work) 4. Find the effective dose

Minimum Inhibitory Concentration (MIC)

  • MIC = the lowest concentration of an antibiotic that prevents visible growth of the bacterium
  • Determined by exposing bacteria to serial dilutions of the antibiotic
  • The MIC tells clinicians what dose to prescribe
  • If MIC is higher than what can be safely achieved in the patient's body → that antibiotic is not useful
Clinical goal: Find an antibiotic with an MIC that is achievable at a safe, physiological dose in the patient.

Antibiotic Resistance HIGH YIELD

What Is Antibiotic Resistance?

When bacteria can survive and grow in the presence of an antibiotic that would normally kill or inhibit them. Extended resistance across multiple antibiotics creates "superbugs."

  • Resistance often develops quickly after a new antibiotic is introduced
  • Currently ~700,000 deaths/year globally from resistant infections
  • Estimated to reach 10 million/year by 2050
  • ~2 million infections and ~35,900 deaths/year in the US alone (2019 CDC data)

How Does Resistance Arise?

  • Mutations within the cell (spontaneous or stress-induced, e.g. error-prone repair from Lecture 14)
  • Horizontal gene transfer — acquiring resistance genes from other bacteria via:
    • Transformation, conjugation, or transduction (Lecture 12)
    • Plasmids, transposons, and phages can carry resistance genes

4 General Mechanisms of Resistance

#MechanismHow It WorksDetails
1Decreased PermeabilityReduce entry of antibiotic into the cellReduce number/size of outer membrane pores; primarily a gram-negative tactic
2Target Site AlterationChange the antibiotic's binding site so it no longer bindsCan involve changes to DNA, lipid, or protein targets
3Enzyme InactivationDestroy the antibiotic before it worksBacteria produce enzymes that break the antibiotic apart (e.g. beta-lactamases destroy beta-lactam ring)
4Efflux PumpsPump the antibiotic out of the cellOverexpression of efflux pumps; same pump can work against multiple antibiotics
Remember: Resistance can spread between bacteria via horizontal gene transfer. One resistant bacterium can share its resistance genes with others that were previously susceptible.

Treating Antibiotic Resistant Infections HIGH YIELD

Strategy 1: Resensitize — Change the Antibiotic Structure

  • Modify the non-essential structure of the antibiotic so resistance mechanisms don't recognize it
  • Most common historical approach to preserving antibiotics
  • Not a good long-term solution — pathogens adapt fast

Strategy 2: Resensitize — Target the Resistance Mechanism

  • Example: bacteria produce beta-lactamases that destroy beta-lactam ring antibiotics (penicillin, cephalosporin, carbapenem, monobactam)
  • Avibactam = a beta-lactamase inhibitor
  • Has little direct antibacterial activity on its own
  • But it inactivates the beta-lactamase enzyme
  • When combined with a beta-lactam (e.g. ceftazidime) → the beta-lactam regains its activity

Strategy 3: Multi-Drug Combinations

  • Use multiple antibiotics instead of one
  • Hits multiple targets at once
  • Reduces the rate of resistance — here's the math:
Mutation frequency = resistant mutants / total population Example: 10 resistant out of 10,000 CFU = 1 × 10⁻³ With TWO antibiotics: Antibiotic A mutation freq = 1 × 10⁻⁵ Antibiotic B mutation freq = 1 × 10⁻⁵ Chance of resistance to BOTH = (1 × 10⁻⁵) × (1 × 10⁻⁵) = 1 × 10⁻¹⁰ Multiplying frequencies makes double resistance astronomically unlikely

Example: Tuberculosis

  • Mycobacterium tuberculosis was a leading cause of death into the 20th century
  • Streptomycin (1943) provided a treatment, but M. tuberculosis develops resistance quickly
  • In the 1970s a 4-drug combination was identified — still used today

Stopping the Antibiotic Resistance Crisis HIGH YIELD

The Pipeline Problem

  • New drug applications for antibiotics are dropping
  • No longer profitable: costs $1.7 billion to develop, only $46 million/year profit
  • Most major pharma companies have shut down their antibiotic discovery divisions

What Can Individuals Do?

  • Practice good hygiene (wash hands)
  • Only take antibiotics when needed (antibiotics don't work on viruses)
  • Take your full dosage for the full time — the full course is needed to kill all bacteria
  • Dispose of any extras

What Can Hospitals Do?

  • Practice good hygiene
  • Only prescribe antibiotics when needed
  • Always prescribe more than one antibiotic to reduce evolution of resistance

What Can Governments Do?

  • Invest in antibiotic resistance research
  • Incentivize pharmaceutical investment
  • Reduce the use of antibiotics in livestock
"A post-antibiotic era means, in effect, an end to modern medicine as we know it. Things as common as strep throat or a child's scratched knee could once again kill." — Margaret Chan, Director-general WHO, 2012
