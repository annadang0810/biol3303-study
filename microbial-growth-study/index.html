What Is Microbial Growth?

  • In microbiology, growth = increase in cell number, NOT increase in cell size
  • Cells grow to a critical mass → divide → repeat
  • Growth is exponential: 20 = 1, 21 = 2, 22 = 4, 23 = 8...

Factors Regulating Growth

  • Nutrient availability
  • Environmental conditions: temperature, pH, osmotic pressure
  • Generation time (time to double)
  • Speed demon: Vibrio natriegans doubles in less than 10 minutes!
  • Without extrinsic and intrinsic checks, in less than a day a single bacterium could multiply to a volume greater than Earth!

Chemical Requirements for Growth

  • #1 = Water!

Elements

  • Carbon — 50% of cell's dry weight
  • Hydrogen, Oxygen, Nitrogen, Phosphorus, Sulfur
  • Trace elements

Organic Requirements

  • Source of energy (e.g., glucose)
  • Vitamins (function as coenzymes)
  • Some amino acids, purines and pyrimidines

Temperature & Microbial Growth

Each microbe has a minimum, optimum, and maximum growth temperature — called the "cardinal temperatures".

CategoryTemperature PreferenceNotes
PsychrophilesCold-lovingGrow at low temperatures
MesophilesModerate temperatureMost common group of organisms
ThermophilesHeat-lovingExtremophiles; ancient lineages
  • Optimum growth temperature is usually near the top of the growth range
  • Death above max temperature = enzyme inactivation (proteins unstable at high temps)
  • 40°F (5°C) slows or stops growth of most microbes
  • Extremophiles are ancient

pH & Microbial Growth

  • Most bacteria grow between pH 6.5 and 7.5
  • Acid (below pH 4) is a good preservative — pickles, sauerkraut, cheeses
  • Acidophiles can live at low pH
  • Helicobacter pylori can live in stomach acid (pH ~2) — causes stomach ulcers
  • Lactobacilli help maintain low vaginal pH (<4) to protect against infection

Growing Microbes in the Lab

  • Fill flask with growth media
  • Sterilize to kill any contaminating microbes
  • Aseptically add a small amount of bacteria
  • Place flask in shaking incubator at 37°C (if aerobic)
  • Return next day → culture is cloudy!

Turbidity / Optical Density (OD)

  • Common way to measure bacterial growth
  • Light (600 nm) passes through sample; detector measures how much light gets through
  • Higher OD = less light through = more bacteria
  • OD is NOT a measure of viability!
  • OD can only measure growth rate, not viable cells
  • A dead bacterial corpse still scatters light (unless it lyses)

Colony Forming Units (CFUs)

  • Samples taken at each time point and plated on agar plates
  • Plates incubated until visible colonies form
  • Each colony was founded by a single live bacterium
  • Only live bacteria can form colonies — measures viability!

Flow Cytometry

  • Uses a flow cytometer to count and sort live vs. dead cells
  • Cells treated with live/dead stain:
  • Propidium iodide (red) — taken up by dead cells
  • SYTO 9 (green) — binds to live cells
  • Samples pass one at a time through a laser beam
  • Wavelength of emitted light reveals live (green) or dead (red)

Effects of Growth Rate on Physiology

  • Fast-growing cells increase in size and mass to accommodate extra molecules
  • Quickly dividing cells are visibly distinguishable from slowly dividing ones
  • Fast growth requires more protein synthesis → ribosomes and RNA increase drastically

Binary Fission EXAM TOPIC

  • Binary fission: cell division after a cell enlarges to twice its minimum size
  • Generation time: time required for cells to double in number
  • Each daughter cell receives a chromosome and sufficient copies of all cell constituents

Fts Proteins & the Divisome

  • Fts (filamentous temperature-sensitive) proteins are essential for cell division in ALL prokaryotes
  • Interact to form the divisome (cell division apparatus)
ProteinFunction
FtsZForms a ring around center of cell (Z-ring); closely related to eukaryotic tubulin; self-assembles into filament
ZipAAnchors FtsZ ring to the cytoplasmic membrane
FtsAConnects FtsZ ring to membrane and recruits other proteins
FtsKMediates separation of chromosomes to daughter cells
  • DNA replicates BEFORE the FtsZ ring forms
  • Z-ring constricts gradually as the cell divides

How Were Fts Proteins Discovered?

  • Researchers identified E. coli mutants that could divide at 30°C but NOT at 42°C
  • At 42°C, mutants grew as filaments rather than individual cells (couldn't divide)
  • Named filamentous temperature-sensitive (fts)

The Min System EXAM TOPIC

Rod-shaped bacteria must divide precisely at the middle. The Min system controls where the septum forms.

ProteinFunction
MinDBinds to cytoplasmic membrane at the pole; recruits MinC
MinCDepolymerizes FtsZ filaments (division inhibitor)
MinERing travels between ¼ and ¾ position; depolymerizes MinCD not at poles
  • MinCD complex oscillates between cell poles due to MinE
  • MinE keeps the center of the cell free of MinCD
  • This allows FtsZ to polymerize only at the midline

How Was the Min System Discovered?

  • Researchers made mutant E. coli strains
  • Some divided abnormally at the ends, not the middle
  • Produced "minicells" devoid of chromosomes
  • Named min for minicell formers
