4 Types of Point Mutations EXAM TOPIC

Disruption of one base may make no difference, or it could alter the encoded amino acid and protein. The genetic code is degenerate (64 codons for 22 amino acids), which is why some mutations are silent.

TypeWhat Happens to DNAWhat Happens to ProteinSeverity
SilentBase changes but codon still codes for the same amino acidNo changeNone
MissenseBase changes and codon codes for a different amino acidOne amino acid is different; may or may not affect functionVariable
NonsenseBase change creates a premature stop codonTruncated protein — usually complete loss of functionHigh
FrameshiftInsertion or deletion of bases NOT a multiple of 3Entire reading frame shifts downstream — completely wrong amino acid sequenceVery High

Why Silent Mutations Happen

  • The genetic code is degenerate — multiple codons code for the same amino acid
  • Wobble: tRNAs recognize multiple codons via irregular base pairing at the 3rd codon position
  • Codons for the same amino acid typically only differ at position 3
  • So a mutation at the 3rd position often = silent mutation

Why Frameshifts Are So Damaging

  • Codons are read in groups of 3 from a fixed start
  • Inserting/deleting a non-multiple of 3 shifts the entire reading frame
  • Every codon downstream is now read incorrectly
  • If insertion/deletion IS a multiple of 3, only a few amino acids are added/removed — less catastrophic

What Causes Mutations?

  • Spontaneous: errors during DNA replication
  • Mutagens: chemical or physical agents that increase mutation rates
  • Base analogs: incorporated instead of normal dNTPs, pair incorrectly during replication
  • DNA modifiers: chemically alter bases (e.g., nitrous acid deaminates C→U, causing CG-to-TA transition)

4 Types of Large DNA Rearrangements EXAM TOPIC

TypeWhat HappensEffect on Genes
InversionA segment of DNA is reversed in orientation (flipped 180°)Genes in the inverted segment are now read in reverse direction; genes at breakpoints may be disrupted
TranslocationA segment of DNA moves to a different location in the genomeGene may be placed under different regulatory control; breakpoints may disrupt genes
DuplicationA segment of DNA is copied, creating two copiesExtra copy of genes; can lead to gene divergence and evolution of new functions
InsertionNew DNA is inserted into the genome (e.g., by transposons or foreign DNA)Disrupts the gene at the insertion site; may cause frameshift or block transcription

Key Distinctions

  • Inversion = same location, reversed orientation
  • Translocation = different location, same orientation
  • Duplication = extra copy created
  • Insertion = new DNA added (often from transposons or external sources)

Compared to Point Mutations

  • Point mutations affect single bases
  • Large rearrangements affect entire segments of DNA — hundreds to thousands of bases
  • Large rearrangements can affect multiple genes simultaneously

Classes of Mutants

  • Biosynthetic mutants: cannot synthesize a required building block
  • Utilization mutants: cannot use a particular substrate

Transposons & Their Elements EXAM TOPIC

  • Transposons = mobile DNA elements ("jumping genes")
  • Major contributors to evolution
  • All organisms carry them; in some bacteria half of DNA is transposons
  • Movement catalyzed by transposase enzyme
  • Transposase recognizes terminal inverted repeats (IRs) and cuts/pastes to a new location

All Transposable Elements Share

  • Inverted repeats (IRs) at both ends
  • At least one transposase gene

3 Types of Transposable Elements

TypeStructureKey Features
Insertion Sequence (IS)IRs + transposase gene onlySimplest mobile element; no extra genes
Non-composite transposonIRs flanking transposase + extra genes (e.g., antibiotic resistance)Extra gene (GeneR) is more likely to be mobile — moves as one unit
Composite transposonTwo IS elements flanking additional genes in the middleIS elements can initially move independently; genes in the middle don't necessarily move with IS

Why Composite Transposons Stabilize Over Time

  • 1 A new composite transposon can break up (IS elements leave independently)
  • 2 However, the middle gene (e.g., GeneR) is typically useful — loss is selected against
  • 3 Over time, mutations inactivate the internal IR between IS and central genes
  • 4 Mutations can also knock out 1 of the 2 transposases
  • 5 Now the composite can only move as a single unit (behaves like non-composite)
  • If GeneR provides a fitness advantage, mutations making the composite act as one unit accumulate under selection

Griffith's Experiment (1928)

ConditionResultConclusion
Live S (smooth) cells → mouseMouse diesS strain is virulent (has capsule)
Live R (rough) cells → mouseMouse livesR strain is avirulent (no capsule)
Heat-killed S cells → mouseMouse livesDead cells are not virulent; capsule alone is innocuous
Heat-killed S + Live R → mouseMouse dies! R cells recovered as SR cells were transformed — acquired capsule genes from dead S cells
  • This led to the discovery that genes are made of DNA

5 Steps of Natural Transformation

  • 1 Exogenous dsDNA binds to competence system proteins on the cell membrane
  • 2 DNA is fragmented into smaller pieces
  • 3 One strand is degraded by nuclease; the other strand enters the cell
  • 4 Entering strand is coated with RecA protein (protects from cytoplasmic exonucleases)
  • 5 If homologous to resident DNA, RecA facilitates integration by homologous recombination
  • Cells capable of uptake = competent (some naturally, others must be made artificially)

Artificial Transformation

MethodHow It Works
Chemical transformationHigh [Ca2+] at cold temperature, then heat shock → increases membrane permeability
ElectroporationShort, high-voltage jolts create tiny transient holes in the membrane
  • Most prokaryotes can be made competent artificially
  • Critical step in recombinant DNA methods

Conjugation EXAM TOPIC

Gram-Negative Conjugation (F Plasmid System)

  • Bacteria exchange plasmid DNA through conjugation
  • Requires a conjugative pilus and T4SS (type IV secretion system)
  • Conjugative plasmids carry all genes needed for transfer

F Plasmid Transfer: Step by Step

  • 1 F+ cell extends conjugative pilus to F- cell and retracts (brings cells together)
  • 2 F plasmid replicates by rolling circle mechanism in the donor
  • 3 A nick is made in the dsDNA of the plasmid
  • 4 A single strand enters the F- cell
  • 5 In the F- cell, the strand is duplicated and circularized
  • Result F- cell becomes F+

Hfr Strains (High Frequency of Recombination)

  • Rarely, F plasmid can integrate into the chromosome via IS element homology
  • Creates Hfr cells
  • Hfr can still transfer F, but often transfers part of the chromosome along with it
  • This increases chances of chromosomal DNA integrating in the recipient by recombination
  • In molecular cloning: regions of homology on plasmids used for gene knock-out/knock-in strategies

Gram-Positive Conjugation (E. faecalis Pheromone System)

  • Transfer does NOT happen via a pilus
  • Mediated by pheromones instead

Transduction EXAM TOPIC

  • Prokaryotes receive DNA from bacteriophages (phages)
  • Sometimes phages make mistakes when packaging their DNA
  • They accidentally package host DNA instead of (or along with) their own
  • When this phage infects a new host cell, it delivers the previous host's DNA
  • The donor DNA can then recombine into the new host's genome
  • This can give the new host a new phenotype

Key Points

  • DNA is protected inside the phage (unlike naked DNA in transformation)
  • Limited by phage host range — phage must be able to infect both donor and recipient
  • Limited by packaging capacity of the phage head
  • The transducing phage particle is typically non-infectious (carries host DNA, not phage DNA)
