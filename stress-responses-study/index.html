Different Forms of Regulation HIGH YIELD

Microbes regulate protein production in response to environmental cues to be efficient with cellular energy. They can regulate protein amount or function.

Modulating Protein Levels (Amount)

MechanismHow It Works
DNA TopologySupercoiling state affects which genes are accessible for transcription
Transcription Controlσ-factors direct RNA polymerase to specific promoters; repressors block transcription; activators enhance it
Translation ControlmRNA stability, RNA thermometers (temp-sensitive structures), sRNA base-pairing alters ribosome access
ProteolysisTargeted degradation of proteins to reduce their levels

Modulating Protein Activity (Function)

MechanismHow It Works
Allosteric RegulationBinding of another molecule changes protein structure → switches between active and inactive forms
Reversible ModificationAdding a chemical group (phosphate, methyl, or AMP) changes protein activity
sRNA RegulationSmall regulatory RNA binds to protein and alters its function
Most regulation occurs at the level of transcription: transcription ON = protein made, transcription OFF = protein not made.

Naming Conventions

  • lacA = gene name (three lowercase + capital letter, italicized)
  • LacA = protein name (first letter capitalized, not italicized)

The LAC Operon HIGH YIELD

A classic example of gene regulation in E. coli. An operon is a unit of transcription: promoter + two or more genes + transcriptional terminator, all transcribed together.

The Three Genes

GeneProteinFunction
lacZβ-galactosidaseHydrolyzes lactose into glucose + galactose
lacYPermeaseTransports lactose into the cell
lacATransacetylaseUncertain function

What is the Substrate?

The substrate is lactose. Bacteria only make LacZYA proteins when lactose is present. The true inducer is allolactose (produced when small basal amounts of LacZ convert lactose to allolactose).

How Does the Operon Detect Lactose?

LacI (repressor) is always being made by lacI gene upstream | v NO lactose: LacI → binds Operator (O) → blocks RNAP → genes OFF | (but a little leaky transcription occurs → low basal LacY + LacZ) | Lactose appears: LacY imports it → LacZ converts some to allolactose | v Allolactose → binds LacI → inactivates repressor → falls off operator | v RNAP transcribes lacZYA freely → genes ON
Key concept: The operon is "leaky" on purpose — the small basal level of LacY and LacZ lets the cell sense when lactose first appears, even though transcription is mostly OFF.

How Does the Operon Regulate?

  • NEGATIVE regulation: LacI repressor binds operator to block transcription
  • POSITIVE regulation: CRP-cAMP complex binds upstream to enhance transcription (see catabolite repression)
  • Both signals needed: lactose must be present AND glucose must be absent for full expression

Exploiting the LAC Operon (Reporter Assays)

  • lacZ encodes β-galactosidase that breaks galactosidic bonds
  • X-gal is a synthetic substrate — when cleaved by β-galactosidase, it forms a blue pigment
  • Used to test if a promoter is active: attach lacZ to any promoter → blue = promoter ON
  • Works in both prokaryotic and eukaryotic systems
  • Also used as a readout for cloning (blue/white screening)

Two-Component Systems HIGH YIELD

One of the most common and ubiquitous prokaryotic sensing mechanisms.

The Two Components

ComponentDomainsRole
Sensor Protein (Histidine Kinase)Sensing domain + Histidine kinase domainDetects environmental signal
Response RegulatorAspartate (Asp) receiver + DNA-binding domainActivates gene expression

The 5-Step Mechanism

1. Sensing domain detects stress signal (e.g. binds a ligand) → conformational change + dimerization 2. Histidine kinase domain auto-phosphorylates its His residue 3. Phosphate transferred: His → Asp on response regulator 4. Response regulator DNA-binding activated → binds target promoters → controls gene expression 5. Feedback: when stress passes, response regulator is dephosphorylated (by a phosphatase) → system OFF

Example: CheA-CheY (Chemotaxis)

  • CheA = histidine kinase
  • CheY = response regulator
  • MCP = the sensing domain
  • Signal transduction: CheA, CheY, CheZ
  • Response: clockwise vs counter-clockwise flagellar rotation

Example: Pho System (Phosphate Starvation)

  • Low phosphate = stress for microbes (phosphate is essential and often limiting)
  • PhoR = histidine kinase, PhoB = response regulator
  • PhoR does NOT bind phosphate directly!
  • PhoR is physically linked to Pst transporter via chaperone PhoU
  • When phosphate is available: Pst transports it, PhoU keeps PhoR sequestered (inactive)
  • When phosphate is low: Pst stops transporting, PhoU releases PhoR → PhoR autophosphorylates → activates PhoB
  • PhoB activates 47 genes: alkaline phosphatases, AMP nucleosidases, phosphate transporters

Pho Feedback Control

  • When phosphate returns: Pst resumes transport → PhoU re-sequesters PhoR
  • Active Pst converts PhoR into a phosphatase → dephosphorylates PhoB → system OFF
  • The same protein (PhoR) can act as both kinase and phosphatase depending on state

Error-Prone DNA Repair HIGH YIELD

How antibiotic stress leads to increased mutagenesis and potential resistance.

The Chain of Events

Sublethal ampicillin damages peptidoglycan | v Rcs phospho-relay senses PG damage → activates RpoS (via sRNA RprA) | + also: ppGpp accumulation → activates RpoS | v RpoS (general stress sigma factor) does TWO critical things: 1. Upregulates Pol IV (error-prone polymerase) • Normal polymerases = high-fidelity (few mistakes) • Pol IV = low-fidelity, generates base-substitutions 2. Represses MutS (mismatch repair protein) • RpoS activates sRNA SdsR • SdsR base-pairs with mutS mRNA → downregulates MutS • Without MutS, mismatches can't be fixed v Mutations become FIXED in the genome → increased mutagenesis → can generate antibiotic resistance mutations
Why mutations become fixed: Pol IV introduces errors AND MutS (the repair protein) is simultaneously repressed. Double whammy = mutations persist.

Key Players

PlayerRole
RpoSGeneral stress sigma factor; master regulator of stress response
Pol IVError-prone polymerase; introduces base substitutions
MutSMismatch repair protein; normally fixes errors but is repressed by SdsR
SdsRsRNA that downregulates MutS via base-pairing
ppGppAlarmone that activates RpoS
Rcs systemSenses peptidoglycan damage; activates RpoS via sRNA RprA

sRNAs in Antibiotic Responses

  • sRNAs are produced in response to many antibiotics, not just ampicillin
  • They can either activate or inhibit cellular processes
  • This is a relatively new area of research with active discoveries

Stresses Bacteria Respond To HIGH YIELD

Types of Stress

Stress TypeExamples / Details
NutritionalStarvation, limiting nutrients (e.g. phosphate limitation → Pho system)
OsmoticChanges in solute concentration in the environment
TemperatureHeat shock, cold shock (RNA thermometers sense temperature)
Toxic AgentsAntibiotics, heavy metals, reactive oxygen species
PhysicalUV radiation, mechanical damage

Blueprint of a Stress Response (4 Steps)

1. SENSING A protein or nucleic acid sensor detects the stress 2. SIGNAL TRANSDUCTION Sensor transmits signal to regulator(s) (e.g. phosphorylation cascade in two-component systems) 3. RESPONSE Regulators modulate expression of target operons → produce stress-responding proteins 4. FEEDBACK CONTROL Mechanism to turn OFF the response when the situation is handled
All four steps are essential. Without feedback control, the cell wastes energy on stress responses it no longer needs.

Antibiotic Stress Specifically

  • Antibiotics target essential cellular functions → loss of function triggers stress response
  • Sometimes the response doesn't help and the cell dies
  • Other times the response leads to survival + evolution of resistance
  • Quinolone antibiotics at sublethal doses can select for resistance via:
    • Upregulation of efflux pumps
    • Mutations in DNA gyrase or topoisomerase IV genes
    • Plasmid-encoded resistance genes
  • Quinolones also increase mutation rates, recombination, and persister formation
  • PERSISTERS = dormant/slow-growing bacteria that tolerate antibiotics until they're gone. They do NOT have unique resistance genes — they just wait it out.

Catabolite Repression (Glucose Preference)

E. coli prioritizes glucose over other carbon sources. Here's how:

Glucose PRESENT: Pts system active (transporting glucose) → phosphorylated Enzyme IIA inhibits adenylate cyclase (AC) → low cAMP → no cAMP-CRP complex → cannot activate operons for other carbon sources Glucose ABSENT: Pts system inactive → AC is not inhibited → cAMP accumulates → cAMP binds CRP (allosteric activation) → cAMP-CRP binds promoters → activates transcription of alternative carbon source operons
  • CRP = cyclic AMP receptor protein
  • CRP needs cAMP to allosterically activate and bind DNA
  • This is why LAC operon needs both lactose present AND glucose absent for full expression
