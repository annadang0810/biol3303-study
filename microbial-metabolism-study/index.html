Microbial Growth Is Directly Linked to Metabolism

  • What do all microbes want to do the most? Make more of themselves! (microbial growth)
  • What do they need?
    • Energy
    • New cell components: DNA, ribosomes, lipids, etc.
  • How do they get it? Metabolism!

Metabolic Framework of Bacterial Growth

Reproduction is the result of a huge number of chemical reactions occurring through sequential processes:

  • 1 Fueling — generating precursor metabolites, energy, and reducing power
  • 2 Biosynthesis — building small precursors into larger molecules
  • 3 Polymerization — assembling monomers into polymers
  • 4 Assembly — combining macromolecules into cellular structures

Catabolism vs. Anabolism

ProcessAlso CalledWhat It Does
CatabolismFuelingDegradative — breaks down organic nutrients into smaller precursors
AnabolismBiosynthesisBuilds small precursors into larger, more complex molecules
  • Metabolism = the sum of ALL chemical transformations in a cell

ATP: The Energy Currency of the Cell

  • Strategies to harness energy are diverse, but they all converge into one universal mechanism
  • Energy is conserved in the energy-rich phosphate bonds of ATP (adenosine triphosphate)

Why ATP?

  • Small molecule that can store a lot of energy
  • Contains 2 very high-energy phosphate bonds (beta β and gamma γ)
  • The alpha (α) phosphate bond is much lower energy
  • Phosphate groups are highly negative — takes a lot of energy to hold them together
  • When the phosphodiester bond is broken, that energy is released
  • Cleaving the γ-phosphate releases 31 kJ of energy (the equivalent of 2 strawberries!)

Cellular Activities Requiring Energy

  • Fueling reactions — getting precursor metabolites, energy, and reducing power for biosynthesis
  • Motility
  • Protein secretion
  • Repair
  • Sensing and communication
  • Even a non-growing cell requires energy

Fueling Reactions & Central Pathways EXAM TOPIC

  • Growth is the cellular activity that demands the highest energy investment
  • Fueling reactions make precursor metabolites from substrates taken from the environment
  • Microbes assimilate chemicals to make the cell's 13 precursor metabolites
  • Precursor metabolites → building blocks → macromolecules

The Calvin Cycle

  • Used by the overwhelming majority of autotrophs
  • Fixes carbon via the RuBisCO enzyme (ribulose bisphosphate carboxylase)
  • Extremely energetically expensive: requires 18 ATP and 12 NADPH per cycle

Substrate-Level Phosphorylation (SLP)

  • A simple way to generate ATP from ADP

How SLP Works

  • 1 Organic substrate becomes phosphorylated (no energy needed)
  • 2 Phosphorylated substrate is oxidized (with NAD⁺) and the low-energy bond transforms into a high-energy bond
  • 3 The high-energy phosphoryl bond is transferred to ADP to form ATP

SLP in Glycolysis

  • Glycolysis is one of the most conserved metabolic pathways
  • 3-phosphoglyceraldehyde is phosphorylated in a reaction that generates NADH and H⁺
  • Another enzyme hydrolyzes the high-energy phosphoryl bond and uses the energy to phosphorylate ADP → ATP
  • In total, glycolysis generates 2 ATPs per molecule of glucose by SLP

The Energy Problem

  • Energy requirements to make protein are HUGE: 29,257 mol of high-energy phosphate per gram of cells
  • At only 2 ATP per glucose, glycolysis alone would require enormous amounts of glucose
  • Microbes evolved more efficient pathways (ETC) to make ATP
  • But glycolysis is still essential for making precursor metabolites

The Electron Transport Chain (ETC)

"Life consists of electrons looking for a place to rest" — Albert Szent-Györgyi, Nobel laureate

Electrons & Energy

  • All energy in living systems is derived from the movement of electrons down an energy gradient
  • Chemotrophs: electrons move from high energy in one molecule to lower energy in another → makes ATP
  • Phototrophs: light excites electrons to higher energy, then they travel downhill → makes ATP

How the ETC Works

  • Bacteria use the ETC and ATP synthase to make lots of ATP and to oxidize NADH back to NAD⁺
  • Electrons and protons are extracted from NADH at the membrane
  • Electrons are transferred sequentially to membrane proteins with increasing reduction potential
  • Protons are exported across the inner membrane to generate the proton motive force (PMF)
  • The ETC is located in the cytoplasmic membrane of bacteria (not mitochondria)

Reduction Potential

  • If protein B has a higher reduction potential (E0') than protein A, then protein A is a better electron donor and protein B is a better electron acceptor
  • Electrons flow from lower to higher reduction potential

ATP Synthase

  • Multi-subunit enzyme with two large complexes:
ComplexLocationFunction
F₀In the membraneForms a proton channel
F₁Interior (cytoplasm)Catalytic site; makes ATP from ADP

How It Works

  • Passage of 3–4 protons from exterior to interior through F₀ rotates the axle
  • Rotation of the axle drives F₁ to form one ATP molecule from ADP
  • Reaction can occur in either direction depending on ATP concentration and ion gradient
