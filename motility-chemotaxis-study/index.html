Flagellar Motility

  • Bacteria can have polar flagella and/or peritrichous flagella
  • Flagella rotate using energy from the flow of protons (proton motive force)
  • Can rotate either clockwise (CW) or counterclockwise (CCW)

How Rotation Controls Movement

  • Counterclockwise (CCW) rotation → flagella entwine into bundles → forward motion (run)
  • All flagella are synchronized to rotate in the same direction
  • Clockwise (CW) rotation → bundles dissociate → cell tumbles in place
  • Bacteria alternate between CCW and CW rotation to change direction

Proton Turbine Mechanism

  • Protons translocated through the Mot complex (~1200 protons per flagellum rotation)
  • Protons exert electrostatic forces on helically arranged charges on rotor proteins
  • Alternating attractions between positive and negative charges on the rotor cause the basal body to rotate
  • Speed is set by the proton flow rate

How Fast?

  • Flagella are long, thin appendages (15–20 nm wide) that act as tiny rotating machines
  • Rotational speed adjusts relative to the strength of the proton motive force
  • Can rotate up to 1,000 revolutions per second
  • Speeds up to 60 cell lengths/second (a cheetah only does 25 body lengths/second!)
  • Scaled to a 6 ft human, that would be ~245 MPH

Random Walk vs Directed Movement EXAM TOPIC

Random Walk

  • Without any signal to direct it, a bacterium will move in whatever direction it happens to be facing when CCW rotation starts again
  • This is called a random walk — alternating runs and tumbles with no net directionality
  • Tumbles reorient the cell randomly, so the next run goes in a random new direction

Directed Movement (Biased Random Walk)

  • However, purposeful swimming does occur
  • Bacteria can move towards growth-promoting chemicals and away from toxic chemicals
  • This process is called chemotaxis
  • Chemotaxis directs movement by controlling the frequency of runs and tumbles
  • Productive runs towards an attractant are prolonged
  • Neutral and counterproductive runs are shortened
  • Result: net movement towards the chemical attractant via a biased random walk

Chemotaxis & Chemosensing EXAM TOPIC

Chemoreceptors (MCPs)

  • Chemosensing bacteria have arrays of chemoreceptors along one cell pole
  • These receptors are called methyl-accepting chemotaxis proteins (MCPs)
  • MCPs are in hexameric complexes aligned on the membrane
  • Held together by adapter protein CheW and histidine kinase CheA
  • MCPs continuously measure concentrations of chemicals in the environment
  • Transmit signals to control flagellar rotation

How MCPs Work

  • Chemicals diffusing into the periplasm are recognized and bound by transmembrane receptors within the MCP array (Tsr, Tar, Trg, etc.)
  • Each MCP receptor detects a different attractant or repellent
  • Binding causes conformational changes that change the activity of histidine kinase CheA

CheA–CheY Two-Component System

  • CheA = histidine kinase
  • CheY = response regulator

In the ABSENCE of attractant:

  • 1 MCP receptor is unbound
  • 2 CheA auto-phosphorylates
  • 3 Phosphate is passed to CheY → P-CheY
  • 4 P-CheY induces clockwise rotation of the flagellar motor
  • 5 Bacterium tumbles

Sensing an Attractant & Accommodation EXAM TOPIC

In the PRESENCE of attractant:

  • 1 MCP receptor binds the attractant
  • 2 Conformational change inhibits CheA kinase activity → flagellar motor free to rotate in default CCW direction (run)
  • 3 At the same time, CheR methylates the MCP receptor, giving it "chemical memory"
  • 4 This resets MCP so it can only activate again at higher concentrations of attractant

Accommodation

  • The process of resetting the MCP so it can only be activated at higher concentrations is called accommodation
  • This concept is conserved in humans — e.g., getting "used to" smells
  • Methylation of MCP means only a higher concentration of chemoattractant will trigger a run
  • Bacteria only move to "greener pastures"
  • Allows the bacterium to move up concentration gradients and stay put when it has found the maximum — all without a synapse!

KEY DISTINCTION: Accommodation does NOT occur with repellents!

With repellents, the sensitivity of the MCP receptors is maintained — bacteria always remain sensitive to danger.

Sensing a Repellent EXAM TOPIC

In the PRESENCE of a repellent:

  • 1 MCP receptor binds the repellent
  • 2 Conformational change stimulates CheA histidine kinase activity → more P-CheY
  • 3 More P-CheY increases the frequency of CW rotation and tumbles
  • 4 Increased CheA activity also phosphorylates CheB
  • 5 P-CheB counteracts methylation so that the sensitivity of MCP receptors is maintained

Why Chemotaxis Matters

  • Survival mechanism — escape toxic substances
  • Growth-promoting — helps bacteria find food
  • Virulence factor — helps bacteria colonize a host (e.g., Helicobacter pylori chemotaxes toward the stomach wall)
  • Persistence factor — helps colonize surfaces to form biofilms, making bacteria resistant to environmental stress

4 Methods of Bacterial Motility EXAM TOPIC

MethodDefinitionMechanismSpeedSurface
Gliding Rod-shaped bacteria translocate on surfaces without flagella, cilia, or pili Motor moves cell adhesions on surface; exact mechanism not fully understood ~2 µm/s Surfaces
Twitching Flagella-independent translocation using type IV pili Pili act like a grappling hook: extend, tether, retract ~0.5–1 µm/s Moist surfaces
Swimming Individual cell movement powered by rotating flagella Flagella rotate CW (tumble) and CCW (forward) Up to 100 µm/s Liquid
Swarming Rapid multicellular surface movement powered by rotating flagella Cells collectively "decide" to swarm; involves specific gene regulation Rapid Surface (needs some liquid)

Gliding Motility

  • Uses a motor to move cell adhesions on the cell surface
  • Flavobacterium johnsoniae (0.4 µm wide, 5 µm long) moves at 2 µm/s
  • Bacteroidetes, Myxococcus, Mycoplasma, and Cyanobacteria all use gliding BUT their machineries are not all the same
  • Gliding is a catch-all term for moving on a surface along the long axis without flagella or pili

Twitching Motility

  • Polar type IV pili act like a grappling hook: extend, tether to surface, retract
  • Slower at ~0.5–1 µm/s
  • Multiple pili can be used to alter direction
  • Used by Pseudomonas aeruginosa, Neisseria gonorrhoeae, Myxococcus

Swimming Motility

  • Requires flagella that rotate CW (tumble) and CCW (forward)
  • Bdellovibrio bacteriovorus (0.5 µm × 2.5 µm) moves at 100 µm/s — Guinness World Record holder!

Swarming Motility

  • Rapid multicellular surface movement — rod-shaped flagellated bacteria migrate en masse
  • Swarming is NOT group swimming — cells collectively "decide" to swarm
  • Involves regulation of chemo-sensory mechanisms, flagella synthesis, and depression of cell division

Myxococcus xanthus — The "Wolfpack" Bacterium

  • Uses both gliding and twitching motility with two separate engines
  • Gliding engine secretes "slime" for following bacteria to track
  • Slime is an extracellular matrix (ECM) composed of polysaccharides, proteins, and DNA
  • Preys on other bacteria through swarming using lytic enzymes and antibiotics
  • Known as the "wolfpack" bacteria because they hunt collectively

Unique Life Cycle

  • Can go from vegetative growth → swarming → attacking other cells
  • When starved, they build millimeter-long fruiting bodies filled with differentiated, reproductive, and environmentally resistant cells called myxospores
  • Two additional subpopulations:
    • Monolayer of aligned non-reproductive peripheral rods
    • Cells that undergo altruistic obligatory autolysis (programmed cell death)

Multiple Modes of Motility

  • Many organisms have multiple forms of motility
  • Myxococcus uses both gliding and twitching
  • Some forms of motility are better for particular surfaces
