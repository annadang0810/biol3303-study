Major Building Blocks for a Gram-Negative Bacterium

  • A microbial cell cannot make its macromolecules and therefore grow unless it has a complete array of building blocks
  • If the cell cannot make one of the building blocks, it must acquire it from the environment
  • Most building blocks can be transported inside of cells (at an energy cost)

The 4 Major Classes of Building Blocks

  • Nucleotides — for DNA and RNA
  • Amino acids — for proteins
  • Sugars — for carbohydrates and cell wall
  • Fatty acids — for lipids and membranes

From Precursors to Macromolecules

  • There are actually 75–100 building blocks consisting of members of these 4 major classes plus vitamins and cofactors
  • 13 precursor metabolites → building blocks → macromolecules → cellular structures
  • There are 100s of complex biosynthetic reactions that must occur to convert the 13 precursor metabolites into cellular components

Ability to Make Building Blocks Defines Nutritional Needs

  • Different bacteria can make different building blocks
  • This determines what they need from the environment

About S. agalactiae ("Group B Strep")

  • Gram-positive cocci
  • Part of the normal microbiota of the gastrointestinal tract and vagina
  • Causes infections in neonates, newborns, and immunocompromised adults
  • Both are heterotrophs, but have different requirements for major building blocks

Precursor Metabolites & the 3 Steps of Biological Fueling

  • Role of fueling reactions: produce 13 precursor metabolites and provide energy (ATP) and reducing power (NADH) needed for biosynthesis

The 3 Steps

  • 1 Entry — Substrates enter by diffusion or active transport
  • 2 Feeder pathways — Generate intermediate organic molecules needed for central metabolism
  • 3 Central pathways — Make the 13 precursor metabolites common to all organisms

Why This Matters

  • Without all 13 precursor metabolites, a cell cannot carry out biosynthesis
  • These precursor metabolites are the starting materials for ALL building blocks

How Do Metabolic Substrates Enter the Cell?

Example: Gram-negative bacteria — molecules must pass the outer membrane (OM) and then inner membrane.

  • The hydrophobic OM presents an impenetrable barrier to water and dissolved ions
  • OM equipped with transport proteins
  • Molecules pass by diffusion, facilitated diffusion, and energy-coupled transport

Getting Through the Outer Membrane

  • Water and hydrophilic solutes diffuse readily into the periplasm if they are no larger than 600–700 daltons
  • Sugars, amino acids, and most ions can diffuse through porins to the periplasm
  • Peptidoglycan does not readily affect diffusion of hydrophilic compounds (Gram- or Gram+)

Getting Through the Inner Membrane

  • Few molecules can passively diffuse through the cell (inner) membrane
  • Only water, oxygen, carbon dioxide, and ammonia can pass by simple diffusion

Active Transport

  • Other molecules require active transport to pass the cell membrane — requires energy
  • Works against the concentration gradient
  • Can concentrate molecules inside the cell up to 1000x!

The PTS System of E. coli EXAM TOPIC

  • Not found in all bacteria
  • Responsible for acquisition of a variety of sugars
  • Energy is expended not for transport but for the formation of an intracellular derivative that is membrane impermeable and now trapped within the cell

Why PTS Is Energetically "Free"

  • The phosphorylation step makes glucose transport by PTS energetically free
  • Because the first step of glycolysis is the formation of glucose-6-phosphate, which would require ATP anyway!
  • PTS is a strategy common amongst energetically-challenged microbes like those living in the absence of oxygen (e.g., E. coli in your gut)

Specialized Transporters: Iron

  • Iron is usually limiting in the environment
  • Iron is essential for both eukaryotes and prokaryotes
  • Bacteria secrete powerful iron chelators called siderophores (e.g., enterochelin)
  • Once bound to iron, enterochelin is recognized by a special transport system of 8 proteins and is actively transported inside the cell
  • Energy demanding process driven by proton motive force and ATP hydrolysis
  • Inside the cell, iron is cleaved from enterochelin and converted to a soluble form

Central Fueling Pathways & the 13 Precursor Metabolites EXAM TOPIC

  • Three most common central pathways: glycolysis, pentose phosphate cycle, and the TCA cycle
  • Widely distributed amongst cellular organisms
  • Together account for the synthesis of the 13 precursor metabolites
  • Some organisms also have specialized auxiliary pathways of central metabolism

Pathways for Making Building Blocks

  • 13 precursor metabolites go on to make the major classes of building blocks: nucleotides, amino acids, sugars, and fatty acids
  • Specific central pathways generate different classes of building blocks

Aromatic Amino Acids & Experimental Design EXAM TOPIC

How Aromatic Amino Acids Are Made

  • The pentose phosphate pathway produces erythrose-4-phosphate (E4P)
  • Glycolysis produces phosphoenolpyruvate (PEP)
  • Both are needed for the synthesis of chorismate
  • Chorismate is the precursor to the aromatic amino acids: tryptophan, phenylalanine, and tyrosine

The Essential Aromatics Experiment

  • OBS A bacterium cannot grow unless media is supplemented with Trp, Phe, and Tyr
  • Culture is turbid with aromatics, clear without them

Building Hypotheses

  • Hypothesis 1: Bacteria unable to make the precursor metabolites (E4P and/or PEP)
  • Hypothesis 2: Bacteria unable to make aromatic amino acids from the precursors

Experiment 1: Supplement with E4P and PEP

ResultInterpretation
Bacteria growsE4P and PEP enabled growth — bacteria unable to make E4P and/or PEP
Bacteria doesn't growBacteria cannot make aromatic AAs from E4P and PEP → proceed to Experiment 2

Experiment 2: Supplement with Chorismate

ResultInterpretation
Bacteria growsBacteria could not make chorismate — block is between E4P/PEP and chorismate
Bacteria doesn't growBacteria cannot make aromatic AAs from chorismate — block is downstream
  • Controls: Media supplemented with aromatics (+) and unsupplemented media (−)
