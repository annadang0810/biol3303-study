What Are Protists?

  • Eukaryotic, mostly unicellular organisms
  • Wide variety of morphology and size
  • Approximately ~50,000 different species
  • Metabolically diverse
  • Abundant, especially in aquatic environments

Functions of Protists

  • Together with cyanobacteria, protists carry out about half of all photosynthesis on Earth
  • Single-cell free-living protists comprise the most complex cells in the world
  • Unlike prokaryotes, they possess intracellular complexity of membrane-bound organelles
  • Dinoflagellates can have up to 3,000,000 genes — ~100x more than a human cell!

Protists Shape Earth's Geology

  • Certain protists make insoluble shells of silica (diatoms) or calcium carbonate (other algae)
  • These accumulate in huge deposits over time

The White Cliffs of Dover

  • Formed by shells of an alga called coccolithophore (Emiliania huxleyi)
  • These algae divert significant CO₂ to their carbonaceous shells
  • Form enormous blooms covering up to 100,000 km² (size of Ohio)
  • Eventually divide into motile cells or get killed by viruses, leaving empty shells
  • Shells sink to the ocean floor and become buried in sediment

Diatomaceous Earth

  • Powder derived from diatom corpses
  • Used as a non-toxic insecticide — dries out ants and shreds up their bodies
  • "Food grade" version thought to have potential health benefits (GI-cleansing, cholesterol lowering, skin/joint/nail improvements)

Protist Cell Organization

Most protists have a typical eukaryotic organization with single or many nuclei and various organelles.

Key Organelles

OrganelleDescription
MitochondriaStandard energy-producing organelles in aerobic protists
HydrogenosomesAnaerobic alternative to mitochondria; produce hydrogen
MitosomesReduced mitochondria-related organelles in some parasitic protists
PlastidsPhotosynthetic organelles found in algal protists

Outer Covering

  • Animal-like cell membranes or plant-like cell walls
  • Some encased in glassy silica-based shells
  • Others wound with protein pellicles

Complexity of Ciliates

  • Ciliate cells are especially complex, containing structural analogs of: mouth, stomach, bladder, anus
  • Some dinoflagellates even have an eye-like structure (ocelloid)

How Are Protists Classified? EXAM TOPIC

Protists are extraordinarily diverse. Members in each subgroup can belong to very distant lineages. They are commonly classified by their method of locomotion:

LocomotionDescriptionExample
FlagellaWhip-like structures for swimmingAnisonema
PseudopodiaCytoplasmic extensions for crawlingAmoeba
CiliaShort, hair-like structures beating in wavesEuplotes
Non-motileNo active locomotionPlasmodium sporozoites

Nutritional Classification

  • Photoautotrophs — use light for energy and CO₂ for carbon (e.g., algae)
  • Chemoheterotrophs — use organic compounds for both energy and carbon (e.g., Paramecium, Amoeba)
  • Mixotrophs — combine photosynthesis and heterotrophy depending on conditions

Paramecium: Giant of the Microworld EXAM TOPIC

  • Among the largest and most complex single-celled organisms
  • Inhabit pond water, oceans, lakes, rivers, and soils
  • Grazers: ingest organic particles, bacteria, yeast, other protists
  • Can also get eaten by other protists (e.g., Didinium swallows Paramecium)

Feeding Mechanism

  • Cilia beat in coordinated waves — propel cell and drag nutrients inward
  • Food swept into the gullet (functions as a mouth)
  • Digested in the food vacuole
  • Digested nutrients excreted into the cytoplasm
  • Undigested material expelled through the anal pore

Toxins & Symbionts

  • Sometimes eat toxin-secreting bacteria — kills them unless they develop immunity
  • Immune paramecia carry the bacteria and become toxic themselves, protecting from predation
  • When nutrients are scarce, associate with endosymbiotic algae — algae produce energy from light, paramecia provide motility

Virivores

  • P. bursaria evolved to eat viruses (discovered 1993)
  • Virivores reintroduce viral amino acids, nucleic acids, and lipids to the food chain
  • Viruses make up a huge amount of biomass

How Do Paramecia Reproduce? EXAM TOPIC

The Rate-Limiting Problem

  • Paramecia divide rapidly by asexual binary fission (as fast as every 2 hours)
  • Must double every organelle-like component quickly — requires massive amounts of mRNA
  • The macronucleus solves this: with 50 copies of a gene, 50 mRNAs can be made simultaneously
  • Cannot divide asexually indefinitely — cells become senescent and die

Asexual Reproduction (Binary Fission)

  • Uses mitosis to divide the micronucleus
  • After several cycles, cells will die unless they engage in sex

Sexual Reproduction (Conjugation)

  • 1 Two cells attach through their gullets and connect cytoplasms
  • 2 The macronucleus disintegrates
  • 3 The micronucleus divides by meiosis twice → 4 haploid nuclei (3 disintegrate)
  • 4 Remaining nucleus divides by mitosis (2N)
  • 5 Each cell exchanges 1 copy with their mate
  • 6 Cells separate, 2 nuclei fuse and develop into new micro- and macronucleus

Plasmodium & Malaria

  • Plasmodium is the parasite that causes malaria
  • One of the most devastating diseases worldwide
  • Affects ~300 million people and causes 1.5 million deaths per year
  • Transmitted by biting mosquitoes, found mainly in tropical regions
  • Life cycle starts in mosquitoes and requires humans for completion
  • Complex life cycle with many changes in cellular morphology and a mandatory sexual phase

Life Cycle of Plasmodium

  • 1 Sporozoites released from mosquito salivary gland into human host
  • 2 Enter the liver
  • 3 Mature into schizonts in liver
  • 4 Released into bloodstream as merozoites
  • 5 Invade red blood cells (RBCs)
  • 6-8 Complex maturation stages in RBCs
  • 9 Lyse RBCs and invade new ones (causes cyclical fever)
  • 10 Within RBCs, differentiate into sexual form (gametocytes)
  • 11-14 Finish sexual life cycle in mosquitoes

Case Study: Malaria Diagnosis

Patient Presentation

  • 22-year-old female with flu-like symptoms: headache, muscle aches, high temperature
  • Recently travelled to East Africa
  • Doctor takes blood sample for microscopic evaluation
  • Immediately prescribes antimalarial drugs

Progression

  • Next day: feels worse, suffers from chills followed by fever
  • Symptoms recur two days later

Was the doctor correct? Why?

  • Yes. The combination of flu-like symptoms + recent travel to a tropical region (East Africa) + cyclical pattern of chills and fever recurring every ~48 hours are hallmarks of malaria
  • The blood smear would show Plasmodium parasites inside red blood cells
  • The cyclical symptoms correspond to the synchronized lysis of infected RBCs releasing merozoites, which then invade new RBCs

Key Takeaways

  • Single-cell free-living protists = most complex cells in the world
  • Like prokaryotes, they need all machinery in one cell — but with membrane-bound organelle complexity
  • Dinoflagellates: up to 3,000,000 genes (~100x more than humans)
  • Tardigrades are microanimals, NOT protists
