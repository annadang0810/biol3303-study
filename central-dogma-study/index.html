DNA Replication: Initiation & Mechanism EXAM TOPIC

DNA Structure Basics

  • G-C: 3 hydrogen bonds (stronger)
  • A-T: 2 hydrogen bonds (weaker)
  • G-C content tells you how much energy needed to melt the bonds (important for PCR)

Initiation at oriC

  • 1 DnaA-ATP binds to DnaA boxes (binding sites) scattered along the oriC (origin of replication)
  • 2 DnaA-ATP molecules hydrolyze ATP to energize polymerization into a helical filament
  • 3 The helical filament exerts torsion on the DNA, melting the AT-rich region to form a bubble
  • 4 Helicase DnaB is loaded onto single strands and unwinds DNA in the 5'→3' direction
  • 5 SSB (single-stranded binding protein) tetramers coat single strands to prevent reannealing

Replisome Assembly

  • DnaB helicase recruits 3 primase (DnaG) subunits
  • DNA Polymerase III assembles onto leading and lagging strands
  • Pol III is shaped like a hand: palm binds DNA, fingers bind dNTPs, thumb positions DNA
  • Both strands replicated simultaneously in 5'→3' direction
  • Replication is bidirectional — two replisomes move away from oriC toward terC (terminator)

DNA Gyrase

  • As helicase unwinds DNA, the DNA ahead becomes positively supercoiled
  • Left alone, supercoils would block the replisome
  • DNA gyrase cleaves the double helix at the supercoil crossover, passes unbroken helix through, and reseals the cut
  • Also decatenates chromosomes after replication
  • Quinolones (e.g., ciprofloxacin) are antibiotics that target gyrase

Transcription: Initiation, Elongation & Termination EXAM TOPIC

  • Prokaryotes have one DNA-dependent RNA polymerase (RNAP)
  • RNAP synthesizes ALL forms of RNA: rRNA, tRNA, small RNAs, and mRNA
  • Almost all mRNAs are polycistronic — encode multiple proteins in operons

Transcription Initiation

  • 1 Core RNAP alone has low affinity for DNA
  • 2 Requires the sigma (σ) factor to bind the promoter
  • 3 σ-bound RNAP binds promoter = closed complex
  • 4 RNAP bends DNA and separates strands = open complex
  • 5 Once RNAP clears the promoter, it releases σ-factor and begins elongation

Elongation

  • RNAP simultaneously elongates RNA in 5'→3' and unwinds DNA
  • mRNA synthesis: 45 nucleotides/second

Two Types of Termination

Rho-IndependentRho-Dependent
SignalInverted repeat + string of adenines in templateRho utilization (rut) site on RNA

Key detailA-U bonds are weaker than G-C, enabling releaseRho uses ATP hydrolysis to translocate along RNA

Coupling of Transcription & Translation

  • In prokaryotes, transcription and translation are coupled
  • Ribosomes bind mRNAs while still being transcribed
  • mRNAs are unstable: average half-life in E. coli = 1 minute at 37°C
  • This allows bacteria to respond quickly to environmental change
  • Parade of ribosomes protects mRNA from nucleases

Antibiotic Target

  • Rifampin targets prokaryotic RNAP to inhibit transcription (eukaryotes have 3 different RNA polymerases)

Translation: Initiation, Elongation & Termination EXAM TOPIC

  • Over half of the cell's dry weight is proteins
  • 30-60% of cellular machinery dedicated to making proteins

tRNA & Aminoacyl-tRNA Synthetase

  • tRNA mediates conversion of nucleotide code → amino acid
  • Anticodon triplet recognizes mRNA codon; amino acid attached at 3' end
  • 20 aminoacyl-tRNA synthetases charge ~50 different tRNAs
  • Charging reaction:
    • 1 Amino acid + ATP → aminoacyl-AMP + PPi
    • 2 Aminoacyl-AMP + tRNA → aminoacyl-tRNA + AMP
    • 3 Proofreading ensures correct amino acid is attached, then released

The Prokaryotic Ribosome

  • rRNAs mediate catalysis = ribozymes (ribozymes predate enzymes!)
  • Proteins provide structural support

Translation Initiation

  • 1 16S rRNA in 30S binds the Shine-Dalgarno (SD) sequence in mRNA (only mRNAs have this!)
  • 2 Special initiator tRNA charged with formyl-Methionine (fMet) binds to the AUG start codon
  • 3 GTP hydrolysis allows docking of the 50S subunit

Elongation

Termination

  • No tRNAs exist for stop codons (UAA, UAG, UGA)
  • When a stop codon is reached, the A site remains empty
  • Release factors enter the A site and hydrolyze peptidyl-tRNA, releasing the completed peptide

Differential Codon Usage EXAM TOPIC

The Genetic Code

  • AUG = typical start codon in prokaryotes (codes for Methionine)
  • Vast majority of prokaryotic proteins start with Met
  • 3 stop codons: Ochre (UAA), Amber (UAG), Opal (UGA)
  • The code is degenerate: 64 codons for ~22 amino acids

Codon Bias

  • Codons that code for the same amino acid are used at different frequencies in different organisms = codon bias
  • If a codon is rarely used, the corresponding tRNA will be produced at a decreased level
  • Highly expressed genes tend to use preferred codons (matching abundant tRNAs) for fast, efficient translation

Why Codon Usage Matters

  • Using preferred codons = abundant tRNAs available = fast translation
  • Using rare codons = scarce tRNAs = ribosome stalling, slow translation, possible errors
  • Organisms optimize codon usage in highly expressed genes for maximum efficiency
  • Important for biotechnology: expressing foreign genes in a new host may require codon optimization

Wobble & the 3rd Position

  • tRNAs can recognize multiple codons via wobble at the 3rd position
  • Codons for the same amino acid typically only vary at position 3
  • This is why mutations at the 3rd codon position are often silent

Proofreading EXAM TOPIC

  • E. coli replicates its chromosome at 1000 nucleotides/second
  • Error rate: 1 in 10 billion — astonishingly low!
  • Most common error: mismatches (A not with T, or G not with C)

Pol III Proofreading (3'→5' Exonuclease Activity)

  • Pol III has a built-in 3'→5' exonuclease activity
  • Pol III cannot advance unless there is a properly paired nucleotide behind it
  • If a mismatch is detected:
    • 1 Pol III moves backward
    • 2 Uses its 3'→5' exonuclease to remove the mismatched base
    • 3 Tries again with the correct nucleotide

Aminoacyl-tRNA Synthetase Proofreading

  • Proofreading also happens during tRNA charging
  • After the amino acid is attached to the tRNA, the synthetase proofreads to ensure the correct amino acid is loaded
  • If wrong, the amino acid is hydrolyzed off before the aa-tRNA is released
  • This prevents mistranslation (wrong amino acid incorporated into protein)

Mismatch Repair (MMR) EXAM TOPIC

When mismatches escape proofreading, bacteria have Plan B: the mismatch repair pathway.

Methyl-Directed Mismatch Repair in E. coli

  • 1 MutS scans DNA and detects the mismatch
  • 2 MutS recruits MutL and MutH to the mismatch site
  • 3 The Mut complex finds a hemi-methylated GATC site
  • 4 MutH cuts the new (non-methylated) strand
  • 5 An exonuclease chews back the cut strand, removing the mismatch
  • 6 Pol III replaces the missing segment
  • 7 Ligase seals the nick

How Does the Cell Know Which Strand Is Wrong?

  • Before replication: chromosome is fully methylated at GATC sites
  • During replication: chromosome becomes hemi-methylated (old strand = methylated, new strand = NOT yet methylated)
  • MutH specifically cuts the non-methylated (new) strand — this is always the one with the error
  • DNA does not become fully methylated until after termination

Other Functions of DNA Methylation

FunctionHow It Works
Guides MMRHemi-methylation identifies the new strand
Times replicationHemi-methylated oriCs cannot be re-initiated (prevents over-replication)
Protects from foreign DNARestriction enzymes cleave unmethylated DNA (foreign = unmethylated)

Consequences of Defective MMR

  • If any Mut protein is defective → high mutation rates
  • This is called a mutator phenotype
